What the Huaca?

The fight to save Lima's archaeological ruins

Story and photos by Christina Cala and Sara Olstad

In Lima, Peru, there are more than 360 known huacas and some may still be undiscovered. They’re scattered throughout the city, hidden on residential streets and sandwiched between small businesses. The huacas are everywhere, and maybe that’s why they’re in danger of disappearing.


{

Huaca \wäkə\:

Means "sacred" in an indigenous Peruvian language, but the term is most commonly used to describe an ancient Peruvian site or object.

}

In Peruvian culture, huaca is a term commonly used to describe archaeological sites, but it’s more complex than that. Even though most peruvians think of a huaca as a tall stone pyramid, the word's definition is actually much broader.

"Huaca is also an adjective. You can be huaca and have the condition,” said Jaime Luis Castillo Butters, archaeology professor and Vice Minister of Cultural Patrimony and Cultural Industries. “Huaca is the name that we give basically to all remnants of ancient societies and cultures.

Peru is known as the land of the Inca but it's also home to more than 25 earlier cultures, each with its own rituals, traditions and languages. Three of these cultures, The Lima, Wari and Ichma, all left behind huacas in what is now Peru’s capital city. These archaeological ruins hold clues to understanding people who once walked Lima’s streets.

"Huaca is a complex name ... The problem is that when we say huaca we focus on the big, the monumental, and we leave behind the small, the humble."

-Luis Jaime Castillo Butters

Though contemporary Peruvians can literally walk by history, many of their ancestor's secrets lie hidden beneath layers of dirt and rock that have formed over time. This is because most huacas aren't fully excavated. Instead they look like mounds of dirt, mostly unguarded and only sometimes fenced.

The huacas are not concentrated in one part of Lima. Rather, huacas can be found across Lima. We visited 14 huacas in seven districts.



Some huacas are well preserved while others showed signs of destruction.

The oldest standing huaca in Lima, Huaca Paraiso, dates back to about 4500 B.C. But even that one isn't safe. In July 2013, one of its 12 pyramids was destroyed. Since then, the government has increased security at the site to protect the huaca, but its size makes it difficult to monitor.

The site covers many 50 hectares, making it roughly the size of 95 football fields. Most of the site has not been excavated and is in what is called a "basural" stage in Spanish.

Like El Paraiso, a majority of Lima’s huacas remain unexcavated due to lack of government funding. Even if the government had the resources to protect them, a majority of Lima’s huacas are located on land that is privately owned.

According to statistics from the Peruvian Ministry of Culture, 60 percent of the huacas in Lima are classified as threatened. Details about the precise condition of these approximately 220 endangered huacas are largely unknown. These unsecure sites lack protective fencing, police supervision and other basic security features.




Some of the difficulties of protecting the huacas can be explained by three main issues.

Land

  • Rapidly expanding population restricted by geographical features
  • Confusion over who owns the huacas and who is responsible for their care

    • Money

  • Lack of government money for security and development
  • Question of whether the huaca is worth funding over other government services

    • Education

  • Failure to include history of the huacas in school curriculum
  • Disconnect with huacas as part of Peruvian cultural identity

    The Pumpkin Show has been an annual event in Circleville since 1903. The event started as a way to show the talents of local growers by the Mayor of Circleville, George R. Haswell. The event has grown in size over the years. What started out as a small town exhibit has now become the sixth largest festival in the United States that claims over 300,000 in annual attendance. The first recorded Miss Pumpkin Show competition was held in 1933 and has been held every year since.
